## SECTION 2: FOOD SAFETY & HYGIENE (3:30)

**[VISUAL: Handwashing station, thermometer readings, labeled containers, gloves, clean prep surfaces. Stock footage of proper food safety practices.]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Food Safety & Hygiene"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> Food safety is not optional. It is the foundation of everything we do in this kitchen. A single food safety failure can make someone seriously ill and shut this restaurant down. There is no "close enough" with food safety — you either follow the standards or you don't work this line.

### Handwashing

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "Handwashing: 20 Seconds Minimum"]**

> **Handwashing — 20 seconds minimum, every time.** You wash your hands when you arrive at work. You wash them after touching your face, your hair, your phone, or anything that isn't food or a clean utensil. You wash them after handling raw meat before touching anything else. You wash them after taking out trash, after using the restroom, after sneezing or coughing, and after eating or drinking.

> Twenty seconds with soap and warm water. Not a quick rinse — a full wash. If you're not sure whether you need to wash your hands, the answer is yes.

### Temperature Control

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "Critical Temperatures"]**

> **Temperature control is life or death.** These are the temperatures you need to memorize:

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Temperature chart graphic]**
- **Chicken: 165 degrees Fahrenheit internal — minimum**
- **Pork: 145 degrees Fahrenheit internal — minimum**
- **Hot holding (buffet): 135 degrees Fahrenheit — minimum**
- **Cold holding: 41 degrees Fahrenheit — maximum**

> Every piece of meat that leaves this kitchen gets temped. Use a calibrated thermometer. Insert it into the thickest part of the meat. If it's not at temp, it doesn't go out. No exceptions. No "it's close enough." If a customer gets sick because you sent out undercooked meat, nothing else you did that day matters.

### Cross-Contamination Prevention

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "Cross-Contamination Prevention"]**

> **Cross-contamination will shut us down.** Raw meat never touches ready-to-eat food. Raw meat goes on the bottom shelf in the walk-in — never above anything that's already cooked or ready to serve. Use separate cutting boards for raw meat and everything else. Change your gloves between tasks, especially when switching between raw and cooked products.

> If you drop a utensil, it's dirty. If raw juice drips on a surface, that surface is contaminated until it's cleaned and sanitized. No shortcuts.

### FIFO — First In, First Out

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "FIFO: First In, First Out"]**

> **FIFO — First In, First Out.** Every item in this kitchen gets labeled with the date it was prepped or received. When you stock shelves or reach for ingredients, you use the oldest product first. New product goes behind old product. This prevents waste and — more importantly — prevents us from serving expired food.

> If you see something without a date label, do not use it. Label it or ask your manager. Undated product is a food safety violation.

### Sick Policy

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "Sick Policy"]**

> **If you are sick, you do not work the kitchen.** If you have vomiting, diarrhea, fever, or any symptoms of a foodborne illness, you are required to stay home and notify your manager. This is not about being tough — it's about protecting every customer who eats our food. Coming to work sick in a kitchen is not dedication. It's a health hazard.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Remember This" card — "Food safety isn't one part of the job. It IS the job. Everything else comes after."]**

## SECTION 3: SMOKER & GRILL BASICS (3:00)

**[VISUAL: The smoker in operation, hickory/oak/pecan wood, ribs on the grate, temperature gauge close-ups, meat being seasoned with dry rub, grill with chicken and sausage]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Smoker & Grill Basics"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> Smoking meat is the heart of J Rodgers BBQ. Ms. J's grandmother knew it, Ms. J knows it, and now you need to know it. Our meats are smoked low and slow with hickory, oak, and pecan wood, and there is no rushing this process. The smoker is not a microwave. You don't speed it up — you respect it.

### Temperature Management

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The Sweet Spot: 225-250°F"]**

> **The smoker runs between 225 and 250 degrees Fahrenheit.** That's the sweet spot. Below 225, you're not cooking efficiently and you risk the danger zone. Above 250, you're rushing the smoke and the meat won't develop the flavor and tenderness our customers expect.

> Monitor the temperature throughout the cook. Don't set it and walk away for hours. Wood burns down, vents shift, weather affects the temperature. Consistent monitoring produces consistent results.

### Wood Selection

> We use **hickory, oak, and pecan wood** for our signature flavor. Each wood brings something different to the smoke — hickory gives a strong, classic barbecue taste, oak provides a steady, even burn, and pecan adds a mild sweetness. You'll be trained on how much wood to add and when — follow those instructions exactly. Too much smoke makes the meat bitter. Too little and you lose the flavor that makes us J Rodgers.

### Meat Preparation

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Before It Hits the Smoker"]**

> Every piece of meat gets its designation before it goes on the smoker. That means you know whether it's a **dry rub**, a **wet** preparation that gets sauced, or it's being prepared for **frying**. This designation is decided before the cook, not after. If you're not sure what designation a batch has, ask before you assume.

> Apply dry rubs evenly and according to the recipe. These recipes have been perfected over decades — follow them exactly.

### Timing & Planning

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Work Backward from the Buffet Schedule"]**

> **Plan your cooks backward from the buffet schedule.** If the buffet opens at 10 AM and ribs take several hours to smoke, you need to calculate when those ribs go on. Factor in resting time after the cook. You should never be in a position where the buffet is open and you're still waiting on meat. That's a planning failure, not a cooking failure.

> If you're unsure about timing, ask someone with experience. There's no shame in asking — there's a big problem with guessing and getting it wrong.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Remember This" card — "Never rush the smoke. Our customers can taste the difference, and so can we."]**

## SECTION 4: PREP STANDARDS & CONSISTENCY (2:30)

**[VISUAL: Prep station — mise en place setup, portioning tools, recipe cards, ingredients being measured and prepped. Labeled containers, organized station.]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Prep Standards & Consistency"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> Consistency is everything in this kitchen. A customer who comes in on a Wednesday should get the same plate they got on Saturday. The same portions, the same flavor, the same quality. That doesn't happen by accident — it happens through disciplined prep.

### Mise en Place

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Mise en Place — Everything in Its Place"]**

> **Mise en place — French for "everything in its place."** Before service starts, your station should be fully set up. That means all your ingredients are prepped, portioned, and within reach. Your tools are clean and ready. Your containers are labeled. You're not scrambling mid-service to chop vegetables or find a clean pan.

> A disorganized station leads to slow tickets, mistakes, and stress. An organized station leads to smooth, consistent service. Set yourself up to succeed before the first order comes in.

### Portioning

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "Use the Right Tool, Every Time"]**

> **Use the correct portioning tools for every dish.** Scoops, ladles, and scales exist for a reason. Don't eyeball portions. A plate that's over-portioned costs the business money. A plate that's under-portioned disappoints the customer. Both are failures.

> When you portion the same way every time, the customer gets the same experience every time. That's how you build the kind of consistency that keeps people coming back for 30 years.

### Recipe Adherence

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "No Substitutions Without Authorization"]**

> **Follow the recipes exactly.** No substitutions, no modifications, no "I think it tastes better this way" without explicit authorization from management. These recipes were developed by Ms. J and her grandmother. They are the reason people come here. If you change the recipe without permission, you're not being creative — you're changing what the customer is paying for.

> If you think something needs adjusting — maybe an ingredient isn't right or you notice a quality issue — bring it to your manager. Don't take it upon yourself to change the formula.

### Daily Prep Checklists

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Complete the Checklist — Every Day"]**

> Every day starts with a prep checklist. This checklist tells you what needs to be prepped, in what quantities, and by when. Complete every item on that list before service. If you can't finish everything, communicate that to your manager early — don't wait until the buffet opens to say you're behind.

## SECTION 5: BUFFET REPLENISHMENT (2:00)

**[VISUAL: Buffet pans — full vs. getting low, kitchen staging area, temp checks on buffet items, communication between kitchen and front]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Buffet Replenishment"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> The buffet is the main event for most of our customers. They expect to see full, hot, fresh food every time they walk up to that line. As a line cook, keeping that buffet stocked is one of your most critical responsibilities.

### Respond to Half-Full Alerts

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "Half Full = Time to Cook"]**

> When front-of-house tells you a pan is getting low, you respond immediately. Not "in a minute." Not "I'll get to it." Immediately. A half-full pan on the buffet means you should already have the replacement cooking or ready to go. If you're hearing about it when the pan is empty, you're already too late.

### Production Timing

> **Plan your production around the buffet schedule.** Know when your highest volume periods are and have backup pans ready. Stagger your cooking so that fresh food is coming out in a steady flow, not all at once followed by a gap. Think ahead — always ask yourself, "What's going to run out next?"

### Temperature Verification

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "135°F Minimum Before It Goes Out"]**

> **Every pan that goes to the buffet must be at 135 degrees Fahrenheit or above.** Check it with a thermometer before it leaves the kitchen. Not after it's been sitting on the buffet for 10 minutes — before it goes out. If it's not at temp, it stays in the kitchen until it is.

### Communicate Delays

> If something is going to take longer than expected, tell the front of house immediately. Don't let them get surprised by an empty pan with no replacement coming. A quick heads-up — "Ribs are 10 minutes out" — lets them manage customer expectations and prevents frustration on both sides.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Remember This" card — "The buffet is your scoreboard. Full and hot means you're winning."]**

## SECTION 6: LINE CLEANLINESS (1:30)

**[VISUAL: Clean kitchen stations, sanitizer buckets, wiping surfaces, organized equipment, end-of-shift cleaning]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Line Cleanliness"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> A clean kitchen is a safe kitchen. A dirty kitchen is a health code violation waiting to happen. At J Rodgers, we don't clean up at the end of the day and call it good — we clean as we go, all shift long.

### Clean As You Go

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "Clean As You Go"]**

> **Clean as you go.** After you finish a task, clean your station before starting the next one. Wipe down your cutting board. Put away ingredients. Clean your tools. Don't let messes accumulate until the end of service — by then it's overwhelming and things get missed.

### Sanitizer Buckets

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Sanitizer Bucket at Every Station"]**

> **There should be a sanitizer bucket with clean towels at every station, at all times.** Use it constantly. Wipe your surfaces between tasks. Change the sanitizer solution when it gets dirty — a dirty sanitizer bucket is just spreading contamination, not preventing it.

### Spills

> **Clean up spills immediately.** Not in a minute, not after you finish what you're doing — now. A wet floor in a kitchen is a slip-and-fall injury waiting to happen. Oil, grease, water, sauce — whatever it is, it gets cleaned the moment it hits the floor.

### End-of-Shift Checklist

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "End-of-Shift Cleaning Checklist"]**

> At the end of every shift, there's a cleaning checklist. Every item gets completed and initialed. This includes deep-cleaning your station, cleaning equipment, emptying trash, sweeping and mopping your area, and restocking supplies for the next shift. The person coming in after you should walk into a station that's ready to work — because you'd want the same.

## SECTION 8: WASTE REDUCTION (1:30)

**[VISUAL: FIFO labels, proper food storage, portioning tools, organized walk-in cooler, waste bins]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Waste Reduction"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> Every piece of food that gets thrown away is money that could have been a plate, a paycheck, or an investment in this business. Waste reduction isn't just about being responsible — it's about respecting the product and the work that went into it.

### FIFO Rotation

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "First In, First Out — Always"]**

> We covered FIFO earlier in food safety, but it applies to waste reduction too. Use the oldest product first. Rotate your stock every time you receive a delivery or prep new product. Proper rotation means less expired food, which means less waste.

### Proper Storage

> **Store food correctly.** Wrap items tightly, use airtight containers, and label everything with dates. Improperly stored food dries out, absorbs odors, or spoils faster than it should. A few seconds of proper wrapping can save an entire pan of product.

### Accurate Portioning

> **Portion accurately.** Over-portioning wastes food. If every plate gets an extra ounce of meat, that adds up to pounds of lost product every day. Use your portioning tools and follow the standards — they exist to protect quality and control waste at the same time.

### Flag Problems Early

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Speak Up — Don't Wait"]**

> **Flag expiring product before it goes bad.** If you notice something in the walk-in that's getting close to its use-by date, tell your manager immediately. We may be able to use it in today's service, feature it on the buffet, or find another solution — but only if we know about it early enough.

> **Report equipment issues.** A cooler that's running warm, a freezer with a broken seal, a smoker that's not holding temperature — these are problems that lead to wasted food and potentially unsafe conditions. Report them immediately.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Remember This" card — "Every plate you waste could have been someone's meal. Treat the food with the same respect you'd want."]**
