## SECTION 2: THE J RODGERS GREETING (2:30)

**[VISUAL: Front entrance area, register, dining room entry point. If possible, staged examples of right vs. wrong greeting.]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "The J Rodgers Greeting"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> You learned about the "Welcome Home" standard in your orientation. Now let's talk about exactly how that plays out when you're working the front.

### The Right Way

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The Right Way"]**

> Here's what a proper J Rodgers greeting looks like:

> The customer walks through the door. Before they even reach you, you've already made eye contact. You smile — a real smile. You say something warm and genuine. "Welcome home to J Rodgers! Good to see you." Or if you recognize them: "Hey, welcome back! How've you been?"

> Then you direct them clearly. If they're here for the buffet, tell them where to go and what to grab. If they're ordering off the menu, let them know you're ready whenever they are. If there's a wait, acknowledge it and give them a timeframe. This is also the time to get their drink order — don't wait until they're seated or at the register. Ask what they'd like to drink right away so you can have it ready.

> The goal is simple: within five seconds of walking through that door, that customer should feel like they're in the right place.

### The Wrong Way

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The Wrong Way"]**

> Now here's what we never want to see:

> The customer walks in. You're looking down at your phone, or talking to a coworker, or facing the other direction. They stand there for 10, 15, 20 seconds before anyone says a word to them. When you finally notice them, you give a flat "hey" without looking up.

> That customer has already decided how they feel about this restaurant before they've eaten a single bite. And that first impression? It's almost impossible to undo. And don't be the person who waits until the guest gets to the cash register to greet them — by then, it's too late.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Remember This" card — "Eye contact before they reach you. A warm greeting before they have to ask for one."]**

> You are the first experience. Make it count.

## SECTION 4: BUFFET MANAGEMENT (2:30)

**[VISUAL: Buffet line — full pans, sneeze guards, serving utensils, labels]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Buffet Management"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> The buffet is the centerpiece of the J Rodgers experience. For a lot of our customers, the buffet is why they come. Your job is to make sure it always looks full, clean, and inviting.

### The Half-Full Rule

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "The Half-Full Rule"]**

> No pan on that buffet line should ever drop below half full during service. When you see a pan getting low, don't wait for it to run out — communicate with the kitchen immediately. Tell them what's running low and how fast it's going. Being proactive here is the difference between a smooth service and a frustrated customer staring at an empty pan.
>
> Servers and cashiers should make rounds every 15 minutes to stir the buffet and sides so they look fresh. Check the pans to make sure they are full and look full. If the crust on any item looks dull, flaky, or dried out, request that someone from the kitchen come out and flip the item into a clean pan. Presentation matters — the buffet should always look like it was just put out.

### Cleanliness Standards

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Buffet Cleanliness"]**

> Serving utensils should be clean and properly placed at all times. If a spoon falls into a pan or a utensil gets dirty, replace it immediately. Wipe down the sneeze guards regularly throughout service — they get splattered and smudged, and customers notice.

> Every item on the buffet should have an accurate label. If you swap out a pan and the new item is different, update the label. Customers with allergies and dietary restrictions rely on those labels.

### Communication Is Key

> The buffet is a team effort between front of house and kitchen. You are the kitchen's eyes on the buffet floor. If you're not communicating what's running low, the kitchen can't keep up. Don't assume someone else will tell them. If you see it, you own it.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Remember This" card — "A half-empty buffet tells the customer we don't care. Keep it full, keep it clean, keep it labeled."]**

## SECTION 5: CASH HANDLING (2:00)

**[VISUAL: Register area, cash drawer (no actual money shown), card reader]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Cash Handling"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> Handling money is a responsibility. When you're on the register, accuracy and professionalism matter.

### Opening Your Drawer

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Cash Handling Rules"]**

> When you start your shift on the register, count your drawer. Know exactly what you're starting with. If the count is off before you begin, report it to your manager immediately — before you process a single transaction.

### During Service

> **Repeat amounts aloud.** When a customer hands you cash, say the amount back to them. "Out of twenty." This protects you and the customer from disputes later.

> **Never leave the drawer open.** After every transaction, close the drawer. An open register is a liability and it looks unprofessional.

> **Handle declined cards discreetly.** If a customer's card is declined, do not announce it. Quietly let them know there was an issue with the card and ask if they'd like to try another payment method. Handle it with the same respect you'd want if it happened to you.

### Voids & Corrections

> If you make an error or need to void a transaction, get a manager involved. Do not attempt to fix register errors on your own. There's no shame in making a mistake — there's a problem with trying to hide one.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Remember This" card — "Count it. Say it aloud. Close the drawer. Get a manager for voids."]**

## SECTION 6: SPEED OF SERVICE (1:30)

**[VISUAL: Drinks being poured, food being delivered, server checking on a table]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Speed of Service"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> People come to J Rodgers for the food. But they come back because of how they were treated — and that includes how quickly they were taken care of.

### Drinks First

> **Get drinks out fast.** When a customer sits down or steps up to order, the first thing they should receive is their drink. A soft drink, water, whatever they ordered — it should be in their hand quickly. A drink on the table tells the customer, "We see you. We're on it."

### Keep Your Promises

> **If you said you'd bring it, bring it.** If you told a customer you'd grab extra napkins, or bring a refill, or check on something — do it. Don't say it and walk away and forget. Broken small promises add up fast, and customers notice every one of them.

### Get Ahead of Frustration

> **Acknowledge delays before customers get frustrated.** If the kitchen is backed up, or the buffet is being restocked, or something is taking longer than usual — tell the customer before they have to come find you. A simple "Just wanted to let you know your order is taking a little longer, should be just a few more minutes" goes a long way. Silence is what makes people angry. Communication keeps them patient.

## SECTION 7: HANDLING COMPLAINTS (1:30)

**[VISUAL: Calm, professional interaction between staff and customer (use stock imagery if needed)]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Handling Complaints"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> Complaints will happen. The food might not be right. The wait might be too long. Something might be off with the buffet. What matters isn't the complaint — it's how you respond to it.

### The Three-Step Response

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Step 1: Listen Fully"]**

> **Step one — Listen fully.** Let the customer say everything they need to say. Do not interrupt. Do not get defensive. Just listen.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Step 2: Acknowledge with Empathy"]**

> **Step two — Acknowledge with empathy.** Show them you understand. "I'm sorry about that. I completely understand why that's frustrating." Mean it. Even if you think they're wrong, their experience is their experience.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Step 3: Solve Immediately"]**

> **Step three — Solve it immediately.** Don't pass it off if you can fix it. Replace the plate. Refill the pan. Get a manager if you need to, but act fast. A complaint resolved quickly often turns an unhappy customer into your most loyal one.

### What We Never Do

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Never" card — "Never argue. Never roll your eyes. Never make excuses."]**

> Never argue with a customer. Never roll your eyes. Never make excuses. Even if the customer is being difficult, you stay calm, respectful, and solution-oriented. If a situation escalates beyond what you can handle, get your manager — that's what they're there for.

## SECTION 9: TABLE & DINING AREA CLEANLINESS (1:30)

**[VISUAL: Clean tables, cleared and wiped surfaces, buffet area, restroom door/sign]**

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Section card — "Table & Dining Area Cleanliness"]**

**[AI NARRATION]**

> A clean restaurant tells customers we care about the details. A dirty one tells them we don't.

### Table Turnover

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Clear, Wipe, Reset"]**

> When a table is vacated, it gets cleared, wiped, and reset before the next guest sits down. Not eventually — immediately. Dirty tables in a dining room make the entire restaurant feel neglected, even if the food is perfect. If you see a table that needs clearing and it's not technically "your section" — clear it anyway. Teamwork keeps this place running.

### Restroom Checks

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Policy card — "Restroom Check Every 30 Minutes"]**

> Restrooms get checked every 30 minutes during service. That means you're walking in, checking for toilet paper, paper towels, soap, cleanliness of the floor and surfaces, and anything that needs attention. A dirty restroom is one of the fastest ways to lose a customer permanently — people remember that.

### The Floor & Dining Room

> Keep an eye on the dining room floor throughout your shift. Spills get cleaned immediately — not after you finish what you're doing, not when you get around to it. Immediately. It's a safety issue and a cleanliness issue.

> Condiment stations, napkin holders, and the area around the register should be wiped and restocked regularly. If it's empty or dirty, fix it. Don't walk past a problem.
